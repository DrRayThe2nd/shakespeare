The Life of Shakespeare

  Shakespeare wrote a total of 37 plays. The first play he wrote was Titus Andronicus around 1590. It was Shakespeare’s first full-length tragedy. It is a revenge play where someone kills someone close to the protagonist and the protagonist is forced to avenge them. As he gets closer to his end goal, he finds himself becoming more and more ruthless as he gets closer to his end goal. Shakespeare’s next play was The Two Gentlemen of Verona, in which a young woman disguises herself as a man so that she may follow her lover, Proteus, when he is sent from Verona to Milan. Proteus, she discovers, is paying far too much attention to Sylvia, the beloved of Proteus’ best friend, Valentine. In the end, Proteus comes to his senses. This plot is similar to The Merchant of Venice, As You Like It, Twelfth Night, and Hamlet. Another early play that Shakespeare wrote was The Comedy of Errors, which was about a twin looking for his lost brother which results in a case of mistaken identity. The next writing of Shakespeare was Love’s Labour’s Lost, in which multiple men try to shun romantic love of women on an island while the women decide who to love. In the Taming of the Shrew, a young woman courts a young man who appears to be her tutor, to the dismay of her father who wants here to marry a wealthy suitor of his own choosing. Simultaneously, her shrewish sister Kate denounces and terrorizes all men. Bianca’s suitors call upon Pertruchio to marry Kate so that her younger sister Bianca may be wed. In the end, Kate is happy with her relationship and Bianca turns out to be spoiled. Not much needs to be said of Shakespeare's histories due to their history. In A Midsummer Night’s Dream, a couple get married. There are a large number of side plots, but that is the main plot. Despite its brevity, it is called a masterpiece and is one of Shakespeare’s most popular works. In Much Ado About Nothing, Shakespeare tells the story of another complicated courtship where the man does not know his fiance very well. The Merry Wives of Windsor is about two wives who transfer their mistakes onto a scapegoat named Falstaff. In the tragedy King Lear, Shakespeare tells the story of a family where the mother has died leaving the father and daughter to deal with each other. In Macbeth Shakespeare tells of a man who is otherwise well-intentioned but who discovers that he cannot resist the temptation to achieve power at any cost. Antony and Cleopatra is about a general who is disgraced because of his love for Cleopatra, the Queen of Egypt. Finally, Timon of Athens and Coriolanus are both about human greed.
